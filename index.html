About Us

Little Bangladesh in the Bronx

Parkchester was completed in 1941 as the largest residential development in the country — 12,272 apartments spread across 129 acres, designed by Shreve, Lamb & Harmon, the same firm behind the Empire State Building. Originally built exclusively for white families, the complex featured 171 red brick buildings, over 500,000 square feet of commercial space, a 2,000-seat movie theater, and the first Macy's branch outside of Manhattan. It was, in its time, a city within a city.

Beginning in the early 1990s, Bangladeshi immigrants arrived in the Bronx through the diversity visa lottery program and steadily made Parkchester their home. By the 2000s, Bangladeshi-owned businesses on Starling Avenue had grown 500% in a single decade. In 2008, the NYC City Council officially renamed a stretch of Starling Avenue between Castle Hill Avenue and Unionport Road as "Bangla Bazaar" under Local Law 2008/48 — a recognition of the thriving commercial corridor built by the community.

Today, Bangladeshis are the fastest-growing immigrant group among the top ten foreign-born groups in New York City — up 92% since 2000 — and now make up roughly 10% of Parkchester's population. The neighborhood is home to six mosques, including Parkchester Islamic Center, a former Young Israel of Parkchester synagogue repurposed in 2015. Founded in 2011, Bangla Bazar Business Association unites over 100 local merchants to strengthen businesses, advocate for the neighborhood, and promote Bangla Bazar as a destination for authentic Bangladeshi culture, food, and commerce.

Parkchester — A City Within a City

Built in 1941 by Metropolitan Life, these 171 iconic red brick buildings once housed the largest planned residential community in America. Today, the same tree-lined avenues and commercial corridors pulse with Bengali storefronts, halal groceries, and the aromas of biriyani and chai — a living testament to immigrant reinvention.

Discover Bangla Bazar

A Cultural Destination

In 2008, the NYC City Council officially renamed this stretch of Starling Avenue as "Bangla Bazaar" — walk it today and experience the sights, sounds, and flavors of Bangladesh right here in New York City.

Authentic Halal Cuisine

All restaurants in Bangla Bazar serve 100% halal food — it's the standard, not the exception. From Al Aqsa's chicken biryani and Neerob's legendary Bengali dishes (written up in The New York Times) to bakeries, sweets shops, and Halal Chinese spots, Starling Avenue is one of NYC's most authentic halal dining corridors.

Local Shopping

Fresh groceries, seasonal produce, halal meats, fragrant spice blends, traditional clothing, gold and silver jewelry, and everyday essentials — all from Bangladeshi-owned shops and supermarkets that line Starling Avenue and the surrounding blocks.

Community & Culture

Cultural events, Chaand Raat and Eid Mela celebrations, community iftar gatherings, voter engagement drives, backpack giveaways, small business workshops, and year-round programming that bring our neighborhood together.

Our History

A Timeline of Bangla Bazar

From a mid-century planned community to one of New York City's most vibrant immigrant enclaves — the story of Parkchester and Bangla Bazar.

1941

Parkchester completed by Metropolitan Life — 12,272 apartments across 129 acres, the largest residential development in America. Designed by Shreve, Lamb & Harmon with 171 red brick buildings, a 2,000-seat theater, and NYC's first Macy's branch.

Early 1990s

First wave of Bangladeshi immigrants arrives in the Bronx via the diversity visa lottery program, settling around Parkchester and establishing small businesses along Starling Avenue.

2003

Bangladesh Society of NY moves its base from Queens to Parkchester, becoming the Bangladesh Society of the Bronx — a signal of the community's westward shift and growing roots.

2008

NYC City Council officially renames a stretch of Starling Avenue between Castle Hill Avenue and Unionport Road as "Bangla Bazaar" under Local Law 2008/48, recognizing the thriving Bangladeshi commercial corridor.

2011

Bangla Bazar Business Association founded to unite local merchants, advocate for neighborhood improvements, and promote the commercial district as a cultural destination.

2015

Young Israel of Parkchester synagogue is repurposed as Parkchester Islamic Center — a striking example of the neighborhood's evolving identity and interfaith transition.

Today

Over 100 Bangladeshi-owned businesses, six mosques, and a population that is roughly 10% Bangladeshi. Bangla Bazar is one of the most vibrant South Asian commercial corridors in the United States.

Community Life

Events & Faith

Bangla Bazar is more than a shopping district — it is a gathering place for celebration, worship, and community service throughout the year.

Chaand Raat & Events

Every year, the community comes alive for the annual Chaand Raat Festival — the "Night of the Moon" before Eid — with late-night shopping, henna, street food, and live performances. Beyond Chaand Raat, Bangla Bazar hosts Eid Mela celebrations, community backpack giveaways for students, small business workshops, and iftar gatherings during Ramadan that bring hundreds of families together on Starling Avenue.

Faith & Worship

Six mosques now serve the greater Parkchester area. Banglabazar Jame Masjid, located at 1351 Odell Street, has been serving the community since 2008 and is one of the largest Muslim gathering places in the Starling Avenue area. Jame Masjid holds five daily prayers and two Jummah services on Fridays. Parkchester Islamic Center, housed in the former Young Israel of Parkchester synagogue, was repurposed in 2015 — a quiet testament to the neighborhood's changing demographics and shared commitment to faith.

Community Organizations

Bangla Bazar is home to organizations that advocate for, educate, and empower the Bangladeshi community in the Bronx and beyond.

Nonprofit · Arts & Culture

BAFA — Bangladesh Academy of Fine Arts

A women-led, Bronx-based nonprofit (501c3) founded in 2011. BAFA preserves South Asian heritage through dance, music, visual arts, and Bengali literacy programs. Featured on HBO Max's 'Take Out with Lisa Ling.' Open enrollment year-round for students of all ages.

bafausa.org
Nonprofit · Social Justice

DRUM — Desis Rising Up & Moving

Founded in 2000, DRUM builds the power of South Asian and Indo-Caribbean low-wage immigrant workers, youth, and families in NYC. With chapters across six neighborhoods, DRUM fights for economic justice, civil rights, and immigrant rights.

drumnyc.org
